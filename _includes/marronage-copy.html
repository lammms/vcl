Mahommah Baquaqua

1824
Profile Image

Mahommah Gardo Baquaqua's narrative was related orally to Irish abolitionist Samuel Moore and published in Detroit, Michigan in 1854. While Moore makes repeated intrusions into the narration with his own observations and inferences, what is clear is that Mr. Baquaqua was a man who was profoundly resilient and possessed a fierce desire to return to his homeland. For most of his life, Baquaqua more or less exercised control over his own mobility. The exception to this was the two years he spent as an enslaved laborer in Brazil. Despite the fact that Brazil forcibly transported more Africans than any other country in the Americas, Baquaqua's narrative contains one of the few known accounts of slavery in Brazil as well as a graphic depiction of the Middle Passage. His movements across the Atlantic and from Pernambuco to Rio de Janeiro were coerced, yet Baquaqua's narrative illustrates his utilization of a variety of resistance tactics during his captivity: running away, attempting to commit suicide, and even briefly plotting to kill his slaveholder. After his emancipation he turned to religion, and his affiliation with the Baptist Free Missions provided not only a much-needed source of income, but also the ability to attend college and raise funds for a voyage back to Africa in order to "preach the Gospel of glad tidings of great joy to the ignorant and benighted of my fellow countrymen." Yet Baquaqua's private letters reveal a much more personal motivation to make the trip - he wanted to go back and see his mother again. However, his fate remains a mystery to this day. Scholars believe that he went to England in the late 1850s in order to continue to raise funds for his mission trip to Africa, but he disappears from the historical record after this, leaving us to hope that he was able to return home after all.
